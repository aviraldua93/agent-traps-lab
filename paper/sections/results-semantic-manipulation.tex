\section{Results: Semantic Manipulation}
\label{sec:results-sm}

This section reports results for the four semantic manipulation
scenarios: \trap{Authority-Framing} (SM-1), \trap{Emotional-Urgency}
(SM-2), \trap{Context-Flooding} (SM-3), and
\trap{Identity-Manipulation} (SM-4).

\subsection{Decision Drift}
\label{sec:sm-drift}

Unlike content injection, semantic manipulation does not embed
explicit instructions. Instead, it biases the agent's
decision-making through framing effects. We measure success via
\metric{decision drift} ($\Delta$): the cosine distance between
the agent's action and the ground-truth correct action.

\subsection{Key Findings}
\label{sec:sm-findings}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Authority framing (SM-1)} produces the highest
    decision drift across all models, confirming that LLMs are
    susceptible to appeals from purported authoritative sources.

  \item \textbf{Context flooding (SM-3)} is most effective against
    models with smaller context windows or less robust attention
    mechanisms, as the adversarial signal is diluted by volume.

  \item The \mitigation{semantic-shield} module reduces drift
    significantly for urgency and authority attacks but is less
    effective against identity manipulation, suggesting that
    persona-based attacks require more sophisticated defenses.

  \item \model{Claude Sonnet~4} shows the lowest baseline decision
    drift across semantic manipulation scenarios, suggesting stronger
    inherent resistance to framing effects.
\end{enumerate}
