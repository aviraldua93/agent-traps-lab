\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Protocol-Level Implications}
\label{sec:discuss-protocol}

Our systemic attack results (\cref{sec:results-sys}) reveal
fundamental security gaps in the A2A protocol specification:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{No message authentication.} A2A relies on
    transport-layer TLS but provides no application-layer message
    signing. Agent impersonation (SY-2) succeeds because peer agents
    cannot cryptographically verify the sender's identity.

  \item \textbf{No content integrity.} Task messages can be modified
    in transit or by intermediary agents without detection. Message
    poisoning (SY-1) exploits this by injecting adversarial content
    into otherwise legitimate task payloads.

  \item \textbf{No trust framework.} The protocol treats all agents
    as equally trustworthy. Our \mitigation{cascade-breaker} module
    introduces trust scoring as a mitigation, but a protocol-level
    trust framework would be more robust.
\end{enumerate}

We recommend that future versions of the A2A specification incorporate
mandatory message signing (e.g., JWS~\cite{rfc7515}), content
integrity hashes, and a trust-scoring extension.

\subsection{Model-Specific Vulnerabilities}
\label{sec:discuss-models}

Our results reveal significant variation across models:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \model{GPT-4o-mini} is consistently the most vulnerable across
    all categories, likely due to reduced instruction-following
    robustness compared to frontier models.

  \item \model{Claude Sonnet~4} shows the strongest inherent resistance
    to semantic manipulation, potentially reflecting Anthropic's
    emphasis on harmlessness training.

  \item \model{GPT-4o} and \model{Gemini~2.5~Pro} show complementary
    vulnerability profiles: GPT-4o is more susceptible to content
    injection while Gemini is more susceptible to cognitive state
    attacks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:discuss-limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Synthetic environments.} Our trap scenarios are
    designed for controlled measurement, not realism. Real-world
    adversarial environments may be more or less effective depending
    on context.

  \item \textbf{Static adversaries.} Our adversaries do not adapt to
    the target model or defense configuration. Adaptive adversaries
    would likely achieve higher success rates.

  \item \textbf{Binary metrics.} Trap success and detection are
    measured as binary indicators. More nuanced metrics (e.g.,
    degree of compromise) would provide finer-grained analysis.

  \item \textbf{Model versioning.} LLM providers continuously update
    their models. Our results reflect specific model snapshots and
    may not generalize to future versions.

  \item \textbf{Human-in-the-loop validation.} HL-category results
    measure whether the agent \emph{produces} biased output, not
    whether human reviewers are actually \emph{deceived}. A user
    study would strengthen these findings.

  \item \textbf{Scale.} While 9{,}120 experiments provide statistical
    power for our comparisons, the experiment matrix does not cover
    all possible adversarial parameter variations within each scenario.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Broader Impact}
\label{sec:discuss-impact}

This work has dual-use implications. By publishing adversarial
scenarios, we enable defenders to test and harden their systems---but
we also provide a blueprint for potential attackers. We believe the
defensive value outweighs the risk, as the attack techniques we
operationalize are already described in the public DeepMind
taxonomy~\cite{yuntao2025agenttraps}. Our contribution is the empirical
measurement and defense evaluation, not the attack invention.
